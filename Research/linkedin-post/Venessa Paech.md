What do Sesame Street, urban neighbourhoods and playgrounds have to do with online communities? Turns out… quite a lot.

Inspired in part by the latest 99% Invisible podcast episode (hat tip to those legends), I've been playing with a new framework to help community design (yes, based on swings and sandpits). It’s interesting to me that it's landed at the same moment Australia is banning young people from social media. 

No matter where you sit on that, one thing feels true: we’ve mistaken removing risk for building capability.

The playgrounds many of us grew up with weren’t designed to bubble wrap every bump or wobble. You climbed too high, fell off something, invented your own rules with friends, negotiated disagreements, tried again. That’s how you learned confidence, judgement, and how to read the room.

Online spaces need the same thing: places where people can find rhythm, take small risks, collaborate, mess up safely, and grow.

The problem isn’t that young people are online. The problem is that too many digital spaces are built without any of the things that make neighbourhoods… neighbourhoods. Something community designer and builders know and operationalise on the regular.

So I’ve written something about playgrounds, and why community design should borrow more from good urbanism and less from the whims of broligarchs. Neighbourhoods need stewardship, and our online spaces need community management.
